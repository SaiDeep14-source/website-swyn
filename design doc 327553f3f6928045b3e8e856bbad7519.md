# SWYN — Website Design Document

### Version 1.0 | Brainstorm & Strategy Phase

---

## 1. Company Overview

**SWYN (Skills Which You Need)** is a fractional CXO and expert advisory firm connecting Indian businesses with senior leaders who have 20+ years of real operating experience. The model is project-based and flexible — companies engage experts for weeks, months, or ongoing — without the cost or commitment of a full-time hire. SWYN monetises through a commission on each engagement. The current focus is exclusively the Indian market.

SWYN is treated as a brand name, not an acronym. It is never spelled out on the site.

---

## 2. The Business Model (as it affects the website)

- **Client side:** A company fills a brief form. SWYN reads the brief, handpicks the right expert from its vetted bench, and reaches out to schedule an introduction meeting. There is no self-serve marketplace. No browsing profiles. SWYN makes a considered, human match.
- **Expert side:** Senior leaders apply to join the SWYN network. They are individually assessed before being onboarded to the bench.
- **Current bench size:** 25–30 senior leaders across 8+ industries, all with 20+ years of experience.
- **Pricing:** Not mentioned anywhere on the site. All pricing conversations happen offline. The site's only job is to get the right person to fill the brief form.

---

## 3. Target Audience

### Primary — Clients (Companies)

The site is built almost entirely for this audience. Three overlapping segments, all equally valid:

- Early-stage startups (Seed–Series A) who genuinely cannot afford a full-time CXO
- Growth-stage companies (Series B+) who need specific expertise for a defined window
- SMEs and family businesses at an inflection point who have never had senior leadership

The **#1 pain point** the site speaks to: *"I cannot afford a full-time CXO but I need that level of leadership right now."* This is the primary emotional entry point. The secondary pain is speed — they need someone who has done this before, in India, and can start adding value without a six-month onboarding curve.

### Secondary — Experts (Senior Leaders)

The expert onboarding CTA is present on the site but deliberately subordinate. Experts are never the primary audience of the homepage. The hierarchy is: client-first in all messaging, layout, and visual weight. The expert pathway is visible, clearly labelled, and dignified — but smaller in every dimension.

---

## 4. Positioning Strategy

### The Core Positioning Statement

SWYN occupies a category that does not yet formally exist in India. It is not a management consultancy. It is not a freelance network. It is not a job board or a hiring platform. It is a curated network of battle-tested operators deployed on flexible terms — the distinction that "curated" and "deployed" carry is important. These are not advisors who give opinions. These are operators who have carried P&L responsibility and executed at scale.

The positioning line we arrived at:

> **"Deploy the minds that built the giants."**
> 

This is the hero headline. It is active, visual, and credible. "Deploy" carries military-grade precision — these are not consultants, they are operators sent in with a mission. "Built the giants" implies pedigree without namedropping clients or companies.

The exclusivity inversion line — borrowed structurally from how CRED positions itself — sits just below:

> **"Not every company is ready for us. The ones that are — know it."**
> 

This does something critical: it flips the power dynamic. SWYN is not chasing clients. Clients qualify for SWYN. This is not arrogance — it is a deliberate premium signal. However, the site must answer the implicit question this raises ("Am I one of them?") somewhere just below the fold, through a soft gate line like: *"We work with founders and operators who are past the idea stage and in the business of scaling."*

### What SWYN Is Not

The positioning must actively distance SWYN from:

- Big 4 and boutique consultancies (theoretical, expensive, slow, junior-heavy)
- Freelance networks like Toptal or Experts on Demand (commoditised, self-serve, no curation)
- Job boards or hiring platforms (wrong frame entirely — SWYN is not placing people, it is deploying expertise)

The "Why SWYN" section of the site handles this through three proof points, written as convictions not bullet points:

1. **Only 20+ year veterans** — every person on the bench has carried full P&L responsibility. No theorists. No career consultants.
2. **Curated, not browsed** — SWYN reads the brief and makes a considered match. The quality of the introduction is SWYN's reputation.
3. **Built for India** — the experts understand Indian market dynamics, regulatory context, and the specific pressures of scaling in this ecosystem.

## 5. Copywriting Philosophy

The copy on this site operates on two registers simultaneously, and this is intentional:

**Register 1 — The hero and transition moments:** Poetic, bold, economy of language. Inspired by how CRED writes. Large serif type. Sentences that land like statements of fact, not marketing claims. These moments create the emotional atmosphere of the brand.

**Register 2 — The functional sections:** Plain, direct, simple English. No jargon. No abstract promises. A founder reading the "How it works" section should feel relief, not effort. This is where we earn the trust that the hero section demands.

The CRED structural mechanic we are borrowing: the hero does not explain. It *signals*. The sections below do the explaining. The user's scroll is a journey from aspiration to comprehension.

## 6. Design Philosophy

### The Reference Point — CRED, Adapted

The client shared CRED's website as the primary visual and tonal reference. The key things CRED does that we are borrowing:

- **Restraint as authority.** CRED's dark backgrounds, sparse layouts and confident typography signal that the brand does not need to explain itself. Premium brands do not over-justify. They state.
- **Scroll as narrative.** Each section on CRED's site feels like a new room in the same building. The rhythm alternates — dark hero, then lighter sections, then dark again. The user is being *led*, not just informed.
- **Big type as atmosphere.** Headline type on CRED is so large it stops functioning as information and starts functioning as texture. The headline IS the visual.
- **Subtext does the explaining.** The hero headline is poetic and opaque. The two-line subtext underneath it is precise and clear. Never the other way around.

The critical adaptation: CRED is an abstract product (a members club, a credit score app). SWYN is a deeply human service. We borrow CRED's tonal and visual language but we must not let the site feel cold or inhuman. The "Why SWYN" section and the About section carry warmth. The hero and bench sections carry authority.

### Visual Tone in One Sentence

**A private equity firm's investor deck, given a cinematic treatment.**

### What This Means in Practice

- No stock photography smiling in boardrooms
- No colourful icons or illustration
- No animated counters or gimmicks
- No gradients that feel "designed"
- Whitespace is intentional, not lazy
- Every element on the page earns its place

## 7. Colour Direction

**Background palette:**

- Hero and "Why SWYN" sections: near-black (`#080808` / `#0a0a0a`)
- Body sections (Problem, Functions, How it works): white (`#ffffff`)
- Alternating sections (Bench, How it works): warm off-white (`#f7f5f0`)
- This alternation — dark / white / off-white / white / dark / off-white / dark — creates the "room to room" rhythm CRED uses

**Typography colours on dark:**

- Primary text: warm off-white (`#f0ede6`) — not pure white, which reads cold and digital
- Secondary / subtext: mid-grey (`#888`)
- Muted / labels: dark grey (`#555`)
- Borders and dividers: near-invisible (`#1e1e1e`)

**Typography colours on light:**

- Primary text: near-black (`#0e0e0e`)
- Secondary: medium grey (`#555`)
- Labels / eyebrows: light grey (`#aaa` – `#bbb`)

**Accent colour:** None. There is no brand accent colour. The contrast between the cream type and the near-black background IS the accent. Adding a colour would dilute the premium signal.

**Button treatment:**

- Primary CTA on dark background: filled cream/off-white (`#f0ede6`) with black text — the only "loud" element on the page, which is why it draws the eye
- Secondary CTA (expert onboarding): ghost button — transparent background, low-contrast border, muted text

---

## 8. Typography Direction

**Confirmed direction: Option C — Serif display headlines, sans-serif body**

This was chosen from three rendered options:

- **Option A** (full editorial serif, white background) — felt like a financial publication. Good but too familiar.
- **Option B** (sharp sans-serif throughout, white background) — felt corporate and generic. Every B2B consultancy does this. Loses differentiation.
- **Option C (chosen)** — serif for all display and headline moments, sans-serif for all body copy and functional text. Dark backgrounds for hero and key dark sections. This is the closest to CRED's typographic language applied to a B2B professional services context.

**Typeface direction (for developer):**

- Display / headlines: Georgia (system serif) as placeholder. Final implementation should use a premium serif such as **Freight Display**, **Domaine Display**, **Canela**, or **Editorial New**. The weight should be bold/700. The feel should be editorial — not old-fashioned, not slab.
- Body / UI text: System sans-serif (`apple-system`, `Helvetica Neue`) as placeholder. Final implementation: **Inter**, **DM Sans**, or **Neue Haas Grotesk**.
- Letter-spacing on body: normal to slightly tight (-0.01em)
- Letter-spacing on eyebrow labels (small caps identifiers): wide (0.15em–0.2em), uppercase, small size (10px–11px)
- No font size below 12px anywhere

## 9. Hero Section — Final Decision

### Visual Direction: B1 — Full bleed cinematic city skyline, text over image

Chosen from five rendered options:

- **Direction A** (3D abstract geometric) — rejected. Reads as fintech/SaaS. Competes with CRED and loses on production value.
- **B1 (chosen)** — Full bleed B&W city skyline, text floats over with a left-fade gradient overlay. Most cinematic. Closest to a Succession title card.
- **B2** (split layout, figure at window) — strong editorial feel but raises mobile complexity concerns.
- **B3** (headline leads, boardroom below) — safe and honest, good fallback if hero photography is unavailable.
- **B4** (pure typography, grain only) — the most premium and confident direction. Identified as the direction SWYN should grow into once the brand has recognition. Not the launch choice — too bare for a brand with no public case studies yet.

### Hero Photography Brief (for when sourcing real image)

- **No stock photography.** The image must feel sourced, not selected from a library.
- Black and white, or heavily desaturated — near monochrome
- Subject matter: city seen from above, a glass building at night, a lone figure at a floor-to-ceiling window, an empty boardroom at dusk — all acceptable
- The image must be slightly blurred or atmospheric — it is background atmosphere, not the subject
- A strong gradient overlay fades the image from left (near-invisible, allowing copy clarity) to right (more visible, where the image breathes)
- The image must not show identifiable faces

## 11. The About Section / Founder Situation

The founder is currently on a notice period at Morgan Stanley (Executive Director) and cannot be named publicly yet. The About section must be written to carry full credibility without naming or photographing her, and must be structured to be easily upgraded the moment she is free to be named.

**Phase 1 copy (launch):**

> *"SWYN was founded by senior operators from India's leading financial and advisory institutions. We built this because we saw the gap firsthand — exceptional leaders on the sidelines while good companies struggled to scale."*
> 

This line carries the credibility of the founder's background (financial institutions, senior level) without naming the employer or the individual. It frames the founding as insight-driven, not opportunity-driven.

**Phase 2 upgrade (post notice period):**
Replace with a founder photo, name, one-line title, and a brief statement. The section layout should be built to accommodate this from the start — a placeholder div with the right dimensions, so the upgrade is a content swap not a redesign.

## 12. Trust Architecture (No Case Studies)

Since SWYN has no publicly shareable case studies at launch, trust is built through four other mechanisms:

1. **The bench numbers** — 25+ leaders, 8+ industries, 20+ years average. Real, conservative, verifiable.
2. **The process signal** — the curated match model is itself a trust signal. You are not a marketplace. SWYN handpicks. The deliberateness of the process communicates quality control.
3. **The language** — the copy does not oversell. It states. Premium brands that oversell expose their insecurity. Confident understatement is more credible than enthusiastic claims.
4. **The design itself** — a site that looks this considered signals that the company behind it is considered. Visual quality IS a trust signal in the premium segment.

When case studies become available, they slot into a new section between "How it works" and "The Functions" — brief, factual, no superlatives. Sector + challenge + outcome. No client names until explicitly permitted.

## 13. What We Are Not Building (Phase 1)

To be explicit about scope — the following are out of scope for Phase 1 launch:

- Expert profile browsing or a searchable directory
- A client portal or login system
- Pricing pages
- A blog or thought leadership section (can be added in Phase 2)
- Testimonials (not yet available)
- Video content
- Any animations beyond subtle scroll-triggered fade-ins

## 14. The Competitive Gap — One Paragraph for the Developer / Designer Brief

SWYN has no direct competitor in India doing exactly this. The closest categories are fractional hiring platforms (GrowthX-style), traditional management consultancies, and international freelance networks like Toptal. SWYN sits in the gap between all three: more curated and senior than a platform, faster and more flexible than a consultancy, more premium and accountable than a freelance network. The website must communicate this gap without explaining it in those terms — the positioning must be felt, not argued.
